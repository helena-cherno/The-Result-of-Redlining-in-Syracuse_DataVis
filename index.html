Formerly Redlined Syracuse Neighborhoods Continue to Face Disinvestment, Especially in Vacant Homes

By: Helena Chernosky

May 4, 2026, 11:00 p.m. EST

Vacant Property, 727 Comstock, former frat house on Syracuse University Campus that has sat vaccant for four years (Helena Chernosky)

In the city of Syracuse, more than 1,600 properties sit vacant. The majority cluster in formerly redlined neighborhoods, employees of the Greater Syracuse Land Bank say that as they renovate and deconstruct houses, more vacancies keep emerging within these neighborhoods.

In the United States, Syracuse is considered one of the most redlined cities. In the 1930s, the federal government created an agency called the Home Owner’s Loan Corporation (HOLC). The HOLC was responsible for giving loans during the Great Depression, making a map of what neighborhoods local agents would give loans to. On these maps, agents marked neighborhoods by grades: Grade A: Green, ‘Best place to live’, Grade B: Blue, ‘Still desirable’, Grade C: Yellow, ‘Definitely declining’, Grade D: Red, ‘Hazardous’.

The borders and grades were based on home value and ethnic composition, denying black residents, immigrants, and low-income populations the ability to be given loans, denying those who were not white the ability to obtain generational wealth.

Although redlining is now illegal for its racist and discriminatory practices, the effects are still seen in modern Syracuse neighborhoods that were once deemed ‘hazardous’. Most of these neighborhoods lack green spaces, updated infrastructure, and Syracuse faces a recurring housing shortage and vacancies are still on the rise. These outcomes are a direct effect of redlining policies.

Neighborhood boundaries from the 1930s are no longer the same in 2026. Some of these regions are the same neighborhoods that were redlined years ago, while others borders make them both stand between formerly redlined and declining neighborhoods.

For the sake of this investigation, all neighborhoods that were severely redlined or significantly impacted by the legacy of redlining to some degree are included in these discussions.



In 2012, the city of Syracuse started to deal with the amount of vacant homes, creating the Greater Syracuse Land Bank . The Land Bank forecloses on vacant houses and decides whether they need to be torn down, or if they can be renovated and sold at an affordable price.

Daniel Stazzone works as the director of sales and strategic initiatives for The Syracuse Land Bank. Part of his job is to sell these houses once they have been renovated. In Stazzone’s time working at the Land Bank, not only has he become more knowledgeable on the subject of redlining, but he has seen its effects in neighborhoods first-hand.

“Because of redlining, it spurred more disinvestment, it spurred more neglect, and over time, you ended up with properties that were not maintained, that then were not habitable, so they became vacant. It's just exponential, and it just grows like a virus in these neighborhoods. And we're talking 70 years ago, and that just went on and on for decades,” Stazzone said. “And so one vacant house on lock is going to affect the whole block, you know, and so all of a sudden you've got neighborhoods with several vacant homes on each street, and so it just grew.”

After working at the Land Bank for seven years, Stazzone says he would love it if his job didn’t exist, or if Syracuse were no longer in need of a Land Bank at all. But, as former vacant homes are renovated or demolished, more vacant homes keep appearing within Syracuse.

“The number of vacant properties is not going up, you know, because we're getting so many of them either taken down or put fixed up and back on the tax laws,” Stazzone said. “Just as quickly as we're doing a great job and our neighborhoods are improving, at the same rate, there still are more vacant properties becoming vacant.”

From Stazzone's work in all these parts of Syracuse and interactions with the residents in these neighborhoods, he has seen residents start to lose hope for the many promises of change from the city of Syracuse across multiple mayoral administrations.

“If you've got like two (vacant homes) on the street, it could be the entire street that is affected. So that affects your property value. They've been living with it for decades. And that's why there is a big sentiment, like from people that say things are never going to change, it's never going to get better. We heard the promises before, you know, the broken promises and investment that didn't happen, and things haven't changed. That's changing,” Stazzone said.

David Rowe, Property Manager of The Greater Syracuse Land Bank, is in charge of revamping vacant properties. Rowe has worked in all of the neighborhoods across Syracuse, but he says that most vacant homes tend to cluster in the same neighborhoods that were once redlined. Rowe also says that there is a possibility that these homes are becoming vacant due to how inflation has affected everyone, and has forced many residents to move elsewhere or resort to squatting.

“These houses, once they get dilapidated to the point that it costs somebody too much to fix it, and the prices now are just insane. I think they get to the point where they can't afford to pay for or take care of it, and then they just (leave),” Rowe said.



Syracuse has faced a severe shortage in housing. According to Plan ONondaga, Syracuse needs to be building and maintaining 1,700-2,500 houses per year to keep up with the housing demand. The gap in housing has left real consequences, in a 2026 census performed by the Housing & Homeless Coalition of CNY, the organization found that more than 1,000 people within Onondaga, Cayuga, and Oswego county are homeless.

Rowe's job is returning these homes to a state of being habitable. Part of the job is removing squatters taking over the house, cleaning up the house and doing renovations to return it to a healthy environment. Then the Land Bank sells the house back on the market for someone who wants to do more work on it, and wants to live in it, instead of renting the home out at a high price.

Despite decades of neglect, Rowe says the work being done in these neighborhoods has brought safety and instilled a sense of community.

“We make the people fix the house. We're just not gonna let somebody from New York City or Jersey or wherever buy the house, slap a golden paint on it and rent it out. We don't do that,” Rowe said. “We want the neighborhoods to be nicer, so we make sure that the houses are nicer. And when you get a bunch of them, people start taking care of their properties. They take care of their neighborhood.”
